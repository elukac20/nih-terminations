U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Grant Terminations

Posted June 1, 2026 | Last updated June 1, 2026
On January 20, 2025, President Donald Trump and his administration issued his “Ending Radical And Wasteful Government DEI Programs And Preferencing” Executive Order, stating that any “discriminatory programs,” including DEI and DEIA initiatives, will be terminated “under whatever name they appear.” Further, the EO references “‘environmental justice’” and “‘equity’” plans as needing to be terminated. This EO was then deemed discriminatory and “‘illegal’” by a federal judge.

Preceding this EO, Texas Senator Ted Cruz’s published his report, D.E.I. Division. Extremism. Ideology. How the Biden-Harris NSF Politicized Science,” on October 9, 2024, on the site for the U.S. Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, & Transportation. Focusing on the National Science Foundation (NSF), Cruz and the minority staff of the committee identified 3,483 grants awarded underneath the Biden administration that fell into five “DEI” categories, specifically “status,” “social justice,” “gender,” “race,” and “environmental justice.”

The dataset that was originally uploaded with the February 11, 2025, announcement was inexplicably removed from the Senate website. Fortunately, due to my past project work with three other classmates at Barnard College, I maintain a copy of the dataset on my personal GitHub, found here. While there exist more comprehensive lists published both by government agencies and self-reporting sites, the loss of this dataset removes transparency from the rationale of later orders, as Cruz’s report was later cited by President Donald Trump and his administration in his August 7, 2025, EO, “Improving Oversight of Federal Grantmaking.”

The database published on February 11, 2025, purportedly served as Senator Cruz’s motivation for his report. Considering that the later EO references key points of the report that were found using data analysis of the dataset, such that the claim that more than 25% of NSF grants advanced “diversity, equity, and inclusion and other far-left initiatives,” the removal of the dataset is concerning, especially when considering the findings within my group’s project that found there was an oversight in correct contexts in the grants that were flagged.

The dataset Tracking Accountability in Government Grants System (TAGGS) is uploaded by the US Department of Health and Human Services (DHH), which houses departments like the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and the National Institutes of Health (NIH). While other datasets exist that contain self-reported disputed grants, such as those found on Grant Witness, the following analysis focuses on government-published datasets. Applying the following analysis to self-reported cancelled grants is additionally important, as assuming transparency and fairness in a government that enacts discriminatory EOs and fails to properly document justification for EOs with serious implications. With this same logic, I relied on the “DEI” terms listed in “Appendix B: Keywords and Phrases by DEI Category.”

The current dataset published on TAGGS is found through an announcement at the top of the site stating that the dataset is “[i]n accordance with the Presidential Memo ‘Radical Transparency About Wasteful Spending'" (found here). The document itself does not provide a rationale for why these specific grants were terminated beyond the reference to the memo.
